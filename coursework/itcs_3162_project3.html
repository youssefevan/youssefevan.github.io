Introduction¶

For this project, I chose to collect item listings from the Steam Community Market and use regression to predict their listing price based on the characteristics of each item. For reference, here is my Jupyter Notebook and the full dataset.

The Steam Community Market¶

The Steam Community Market is a marketplace where users can sell collectible in-game items. When a seller sells an item, that item is transferred into the buyer's Steam inventory and the buyer's money is transferred into the seller's Steam Wallet.

Items¶

While the Community Market supports the buying and selling of items from multiple games, the items I chose to examine are from Counter-Strike 2. Counter-Strike 2 has multiple types of items that can be sold on the Community Market, but I will be specifically examining one item type: weapon skins. Weapon skins can be applied to in-game weapons to change their visual appearance, but don't impact the gameplay attributes of weapons. For example, below is a weapon without a skin (left) and with a skin (right).

To obtain weapon skins, players can either open a weapon crate or purchase a specific skin from the Community Market. When opening a weapon crate, players exchange $2.50 for a random skin. Some skins are more rare than others, making them more valuable. Furthermore, weapon skins have various attributes that can affect their appearance and value in the Community Market. Any skin opened in a crate can be listed for sale on the Community Market. This creates what is essentially a micro-economy inside of the Community Market, where rare or otherwise appealing skins are sold on the Community Market for more than common or otherwise unappealing skins.

Data Collection¶

Scraping¶

I followed an article by Dr. Blake Porter to scrape Steam Community Market data. This code can be used to scrape any number of items from any game with a Community Market presence.

Since Steam only allows for 50 items to be retrieved at once, I used a loop to iterate through multiple pages automatically. However, Steam may reject a request at any time, so I needed to save the data at each iteration. The furthest I could get was 1,600 items before Steam rejected a request. I saved this data to a JSON and CSV file to be cleaned.

Data Cleaning¶

Removing Unnecessary Fields¶

The first step of the cleaning process was removing unnecessary fields from each item's data. I removed fields that were duplicates of other fields (such as has_name), fields that shared values between all items (such as app_id which indicates the game that the item is used in), and other fields that would not affect the listing price of the item.

Extracting Fields¶

Some of the fields I was interested in were included in the "name" column. For example, the "StatTrak" attribute is an uncommon attribute for skins to possess. However, the StatTrak attribute in the data is not given its own field but instead is included in the name of the item. To extract this field, and other fields like it, I needed to use language processing techniques.

Souvenir¶

Souvenir skins are items that have one or more professional players' signatures included on them. These signatures can be bought separately and applied to skins. For each item containing the string "Souvenir" in its name, I set their "is_souvenir" field to 1.

Wear Rating¶

Wear rating is a random attribute that is different for every individual item. Items with a high wear rating have a more worn appearance to them. For example, below is the same skin with different wear ratings. The left shows an item that is very worn while the left shows an item with the same skin but with low wear.

There are five categories of wear, ranging from "Well-Worn" to "Factory New." To extract this attribute, I used Regular Expressions to find the string within parentheses. Then, based on the description of wear, I assigned a number value to the "wear_rating_num" column. This also allowed me to remove any non-weapon skin item from the data because only weapon skins have a wear rating.

temp_names = df_clean["name"].copy()
wear_rating = []
for i in range(0, len(temp_names)):
    wear_rating.append("")
    search = re.search('\(([^)]+)', temp_names[i])
    if search:
        wear_rating[i] = search.group(1)

wear_rating_num = []
for i in range(0, len(wear_rating)):
    wear_rating_num.append(0)
    if wear_rating[i] == "Factory New":
        wear_rating_num[i] = 5
    elif wear_rating[i] == "Minimal Wear":
        wear_rating_num[i] = 4
    elif wear_rating[i] == "Field-Tested":
        wear_rating_num[i] = 3
    elif wear_rating[i] == "Well-Worn":
        wear_rating_num[i] = 2
    elif wear_rating[i] == "Battle-Scarred":
        wear_rating_num[i] = 1

Conclusion¶

Results¶

While the final model didn't perform very accurately, I am happy with how much I was able to improve the models through experimentation. Each experiment revealed a different way in which each model was struggling, and I enjoyed figuring out how to target each weakness.

I think that the model needs more impactful features to improve further. For example, I suspect that the weapon finish and weapon model have a big impact on the price of items, but I didn't include these features in this project. Since these features cannot be numerically represented, I would have to use one-hot encoding to use them in the model.

Impact¶

While I find the Steam Community Market very interesting, I think that the effects the system has on the player base is overall negative. Skins can be opened from crates for $2.50 and sold on the Community Market for real money. Since opening crates gives players a random skin, there is a small chance that they can get a very rare skin. Since these skins can be sold on the Community Market for real money, skins have tangible value. Therefore, the system essentially creates an economy based on gambling. While I don't inherently have a problem with gambling, many players are children and teenagers, so this system essentially allows for underage gambling. I find this to be highly unethical and believe that Valve (the developers of both Counter-Strike and Steam) needs to take action to prevent children from participating in the opening, selling, and trading of skins.

That being said, my project's utility in the skins market is limited at best. However, if it were to be improved, it could be used by buyers and sellers to predict the price of certain items based on their specific features. Again, I don't inherently have a problem with this use case, but I don't like the idea of contributing to a system that allows for underage gambling.
